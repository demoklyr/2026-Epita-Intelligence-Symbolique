AlexNet, created by Alex Krizhevsky, Ilya Sutskever, and Geoffrey Hinton at the University of Toronto, won the ImageNet competition in 2012 with a top-5 error rate of 15.3%, far better than the runner-up. AlexNet used a deep CNN with ReLU activations and GPU training. This victory marked the beginning of the deep learning revolution.

Alex Krizhevsky later joined Google Brain. Ilya Sutskever co-founded OpenAI and served as its Chief Scientist. The CUDA parallel computing platform developed by NVIDIA enabled the GPU training that made deep learning practical at scale.

Google Brain was founded in 2011 by Jeff Dean, Greg Corrado, and Andrew Ng. The team trained a neural network on YouTube videos to recognize cats without any labeled data, demonstrating unsupervised learning at scale. Google Brain later developed TensorFlow, an open-source machine learning framework released in 2015.

Andrew Ng also co-founded Coursera and led AI at Baidu before founding AI Fund. Ng is known for making AI education widely accessible through online courses. He previously led the Stanford AI Lab.

ResNet, developed by Kaiming He and colleagues at Microsoft Research, won the ImageNet competition in 2015 with a groundbreaking 152-layer deep network using residual connections. Residual connections allow gradients to flow directly through the network, solving the vanishing gradient problem that previously prevented training very deep networks.

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) were introduced by Ian Goodfellow in 2014. GANs consist of two neural networks, a generator and a discriminator, trained in opposition. Goodfellow invented GANs at the University of Montreal while working with Yoshua Bengio. GANs enabled realistic image synthesis and became foundational for generative AI.
